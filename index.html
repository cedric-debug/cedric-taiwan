Travel Essay · Taiwan

Taiwan Ate Me
Alive

A Filipino's honest food diary: the comforting, the cinematic, and the parts nobody puts on their travel reels.

By Cedric
From Metro Manila, PH
6 stops · One honest trip

There is something about Taiwan that disarms you before you even realize it. It is not one grand gesture. It is quieter. The orderliness of its streets, the cool air inside convenience stores that feel like a civic amenity, the way people walk without the low-level urgency that Metro Manila wires into your nervous system by default.

As a Filipino from Quezon City, stepping into Taiwan felt like entering a parallel version of familiar. Asian urban rhythm, yes. But cleaned up. Considered. The kind of city life that makes you realize the baseline does not have to be chaos.

I did what I always do when I travel: I ate first and thought about it later. And honestly, if I had to explain this entire trip through food alone, I probably could.

"This was not a trip about checking off landmarks. It was about what happens when you slow down enough to let ordinary places become something more."

Six places stood out: Xi Fu Yang, Jiguang Fried Chicken, Chun Sui Tang, Grandma Lai's Sweet Taro Balls, A-Mei Tea House, and Fuhong Beef Noodles. Each gave me a different texture of Taiwan. None of them were perfect. All of them were worth it.

Let me tell you about them honestly.

The Six Stops
01
Stop 01

Xi Fu Yang

Dumplings first, boba right after

Xi Fu Yang, at least in my memory of the order, was the dumplings-and-boba stop. It was not the most obvious pairing on paper, but it worked in practice: something hot and savory first, then something cold and sweet to reset the whole meal.

The dumplings did the real work. They were comforting, filling, and direct in a way I always appreciate when traveling. The boba softened the edges after that and turned the stop into less of a formal sit-down meal and more of a small recovery break in the day.

The honest part: This was one of the places where ordering felt the least clear. Between the language gap and me half-guessing my way through the menu, I am still describing it as "boba and dumplings?" for a reason. It worked out, but it was not a smooth ordering experience.

Dumplings Boba reset Language barrier
02
Stop 02

Jiguang Fried Chicken

Chicken steak first, poppers right after

Jiguang Fried Chicken was less subtle. I went for the chicken steak and the poppers, which is exactly the kind of order that makes complete sense when you are hungry, walking, and making choices based on smell alone.

The chicken steak had the bigger, more satisfying bite, while the poppers were easier to snack on between steps. It was greasy in the best possible way: salty, hot, immediate, and impossible to regret while it was happening.

The honest part: The queue can be genuinely punishing, especially at peak hours. I watched tourists give up and leave. It is also easy to over-order because everything looks manageable. It is not. I ate too much, stood in the heat, and spent twenty minutes regretting my life choices. Worth it, but pace yourself.

Chicken steak Poppers Long queues Easy to overeat
"Taiwan's food does not perform for you. It simply exists, deeply settled into daily life, and somehow that makes it taste better."
A thought somewhere between the second tea and the beef noodles
03
Stop 03 · Taichung

Chun Sui Tang

The original boba, no detours

At Chun Sui Tang, I kept it simple and got the original boba. That felt like the only correct move. If you are in the place attached to bubble tea's origin story, there is no need to get clever with the order.

What I liked was how balanced it tasted. The pearls had the texture I wanted, the sweetness stayed controlled, and the whole drink felt more like a reference point than a novelty. It made a lot of other milk teas make sense in reverse.

The honest part: It is expensive relative to what you can find at any number of equally good bubble tea shops nearby, and the tourist traffic inside can make the atmosphere feel less serene than you'd hope. Go on a weekday, ideally mid-morning, if you can.

Original boba Balanced sweetness Tourist crowds Premium pricing
04
Stop 04 · Jiufen

Grandma Lai's
Sweet Taro Balls

Comfort in a bowl, ego-free

I did not overthink this one. I went there for the sweet taro balls, and that is exactly what I wanted from it: something chewy, comforting, and simple enough to let the texture do most of the work.

As a Filipino, the texture vocabulary hit somewhere familiar. We understand the emotional power of soft and starchy: bilo-bilo, ginataan, all of it. So while this was distinctly Taiwanese, there was something in it that felt immediately relatable.

The honest part: The queue, set against Jiufen's already-cramped alleys, can feel claustrophobic. The old street is beautiful, but it is extremely narrow and heavily trafficked. On a weekend, personal space becomes a luxury you cannot afford. The taro balls are worth the wait. The shuffling through tourist crowds to get there is less romantic in practice than it looks in photos.

Sweet taro balls Chewy comfort Jiufen weekend crowds
05
Stop 05 · Jiufen

A-Mei Tea House

A tea set that forced me to slow down

At A-Mei Tea House, what I actually had was the tea set, and that changes the memory of the place a little. Yes, the setting is cinematic. But the thing I remember most is the ritual: the small cups, the repeated pours, and the feeling that the whole table had to slow down to match the tea.

That part landed harder than the view. Travel can get very checklist-heavy, and the tea set forced the opposite rhythm. You sit, pour, taste, and stay longer than you meant to.

The honest part: You are absolutely paying for the setting, and the famous seats are still crowded. The tea set made the stop worthwhile for me, but the tourist machinery around it is very real.

Tea set Slow ritual Overpriced Crowded views
06
Stop 06 · Taipei

Fuhong Beef Noodles

Mostly about the beef

Fuhong Beef Noodles was the heavy closer, and for me the star was the beef more than anything else. The pieces were soft, deeply cooked, and serious enough that the bowl felt built around them.

The broth and noodles obviously mattered, but the beef is what stayed in my head. It had the kind of texture that makes a place feel earned rather than just famous.

The honest part: The portions are larger than they look, and combined with a full day of walking in the heat, I nearly needed a recovery nap afterward. Also, the location is a no-frills shophouse setup that will feel aggressively ordinary after A-Mei Tea House. That is not a flaw. But manage your expectations about the setting. The experience lives entirely in the bowl.

Tender beef Deep broth Not Instagrammable Heavy on a full stomach

What Nobody Posts About Taiwan

The friction is real. Here is what to actually prepare for.

01 · Language

The Language Wall

Outside tourist-heavy areas, Mandarin does most of the work and English menus are inconsistent. Keep a camera translator ready and expect to point more than you speak.

02 · Crowds

The Crowd Reality

Jiufen is the most photogenic stop on the trip and also the most crowded. Go on a weekday morning if you want the place to feel even slightly breathable.

03 · Heat

The Heat, If Summer

Taiwan summer heat is heavier than it first looks, especially once humidity and hills combine. Water, shade, and slower pacing matter more than you think.

04 · Data

Navigation Without Data

The transit system is excellent, but only if your map is live. A local SIM or pocket WiFi saves you from wasting time between stations, buses, and alley turns.

05 · Pricing

Hype vs. Reality Pricing

Famous spots like Chun Sui Tang and A-Mei charge for name and setting, not just quality. Budget for that premium or treat them as one-off splurges.

06 · Transit

Getting Between Cities

Taipei, Taichung, and Tainan look close on paper, but the travel time adds up. Book HSR segments ahead and avoid cross-city decisions when you are already tired.

Would I Go
Back?

Yes. Without hesitation.

Not because Taiwan was flawless. It was not. The crowds at Jiufen test your patience. The language gap is real. Some prices are inflated by fame. You will eat past your limit at least once and regret nothing.

But Taiwan made me feel good while I was there. Not in a polished, curated, tourist-package way. In a genuine, everyday-life-is-worth-living kind of way. The food, the pace, the streets: they all worked together toward something that is harder to name than it is to feel.

As a 25-year-old from Metro Manila, that balance (accessible but special, vibrant but not exhausting) is exactly what I needed somewhere. Taiwan just happened to be where I found it.
